The Importance of Soil pH

Soil pH is a key factor affecting the availability of essential crop nutrients. Using current soil test values and determining crop removal rates of nutrients like Phosphorus (P) and Potassium (K) can help guide fertility management decisions.

What Can Cause Nutrient Deficiency?

When nutrient deficiency symptoms are observed, multiple questions should be considered:

  2. 2
  3. 3
  4. 4

  • Was adequate fertilizer applied?

    Skipping or limiting applications of P and K can negatively impact yield potential and stress tolerance.

  • Did an adequate amount of nutrients remain in the intended area?

    N is particularly susceptible to movement, tie-up and loss.

  • Did the plant have adequate root growth and soil conditions to access applied nutrients?

    Many corn-on-corn fields can have increased compaction due to increased equipment traffic and soils that dry more slowly in the spring. Roots restricted in the vegetative stage generally lack health and often have a decreased ability to absorb nutrients and moisture.

  • Did something else outcompete the crop for the nutrients intended for the crop (i.e. did something such as immobilization occur)?

    Soil microbes, which help decompose crop residue, as well as weeds can outcompete crops for nutrients. Historically, corn-on-corn acres have suffered N deficiencies likely due to the immobilization of N with residue decomposition.
