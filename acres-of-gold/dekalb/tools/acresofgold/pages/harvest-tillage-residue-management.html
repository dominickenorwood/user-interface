Timely Residue Management

Clear management practices for decomposing residue may improve the ability of the corn crop to overcome challenges of heavy residue.

  1. Harvest Corn-on-Corn First
  2. Combine Spreaders
  3. Stalk Choppers
  4. Vertical / Aggressive Tillage

  • Harvest Corn-on-Corn First

    Harvest fields to be planted back to corn next year first to help maximize the amount of time for residue decomposition prior to cold winter temperatures which limit soil microbial activity.

  • Combine Spreaders

    Combine spreaders can help distribute residue evenly.

  • Stalk Chopper

    Chopping stalks into smaller pieces can aid in residue break down, but having a mat of residue may make planting more difficult.

  • Vertical / Aggressive Tillage

    “Vertical” tillage, not subsoiling but rather a tool comprised of disks with notches that runs parallel with the tractor can be used to “mulch” residue. Aggressive tillage can help maximize contact of residue and soil. Tilling, aggressive or “vertical”, as soon as possible after harvest can help take advantage of warmer weather and increased microbial activity. Often tillage can be done in the fall and the spring. To prevent plugging or clogging, the implement should be set properly and the soil should not be too wet.

Source

Feeding Treated Corn Stover

New treatment processes can transform corn stover into an alternative feed source that can replace some of the corn grain and hay in your ration. Also feeds very well with co-products from ethanol.
