Disease Management

Distinguishing Corn Ear Molds

Pathogens that cause ear rot diseases survive on corn residue and are of greater concern in continuous corn. Though actual disease infection may occur as early as silking, corn ear molds first become visually apparent on developing corn ears during grain fill. Use the slider below to learn about some of the most common ear molds affecting corn.

  1. Asperg­illus
  2. Dip­lodia
  3. Fus­arium
  4. Gibber­ella
  5. Peni­cillium
  6. Trich­oderma
  7. Clado­sporium

  • Aspergillus

    Color

    Gray-green; light green

    Appearance

    Powdery mold starting at tip of ear

    Conditions

    Damaged silks or kernels typically from insects or hail; common in dry years

    Mycotoxin

    Aflatoxin: toxic to livestock and humans

  • Diplodia

    Color

    White to Grey; severe infection can cause entire ear to appear brown

    Appearance

    Usually begins at base of ear and develops toward the tip; grows between kernels; often pycnidia will form on husks and at the base of the kernels

    Conditions

    Most often in reduced tillage and continous corn

    Mycotoxin

    Not known to produce mycotoxins

  • Fusarium

    Color

    White to pink

    Appearance

    Individual kernels with fungal growth and/or kernels with starburst pattern

    Conditions

    Infection points include kernel growth cracks and ear damage from insects; warm and dry weather favors disease development

    Mycotoxin

    Fumonisin: toxic to livestock, particularly horses

  • Gibberella

    Color

    Often bright pink; varies from red to white

    Appearance

    Usually begins at ear tips and progressed to base

    Conditions

    Infection favored by cool, wet weather after silking

    Mycotoxin

    Vomitoxin, zearalenone: harmful to livestock

  • Penicillium

    Color

    Blue green

    Appearance

    Grows on and between kernels; powdery

    Conditions

    Infects kernels damaged by frost, insects or hail

    Mycotoxin

    Not known to produce mycotoxins

  • Trichoderma

    Color

    Green

    Appearance

    Grows on and between husks and kernels

    Conditions

    Favored by insect or mechanical damage to the ear

    Mycotoxin

    Not known to produce mycotoxin

  • Cladosporium

    Color

    Gray to black or very dark green

    Appearance

    Streaks scattered over ear; can appear powdery; able to rub color off kernel surface

    Conditions

    Infects kernels damaged by frost, insects or hail

    Mycotoxin

    No feeding toxicity
